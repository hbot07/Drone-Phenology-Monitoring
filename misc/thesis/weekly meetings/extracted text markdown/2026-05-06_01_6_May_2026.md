## Slide 3

- Core Idea

- Use drone images as ground truth to label coarse satellite pixels, then train an ML model that can predict fine-grained phenological signals from satellite time series alone

- Flowering (white/cream stamens appearing on green canopy); lasts only a few weeks per year, January–April

## Slide 4

- ML Task: Random Forest Regression

- Prediction target (label/y): The proportion of a satellite pixel's footprint that is covered by flowers (0–100%), on a specific day tx

- It is derived from the drone: for each 6×6 m satellite pixel, they count what fraction of the 2 cm drone pixels inside a 14×14 m buffer around it are classified as "Flower"

- Predictor variables (features/X): 21 features, all derived from the satellite GNDVI and EBI time series at specific phenologically meaningful dates. These are described in detail below.

## Slide 5

- Drone Data (label generator)

- 2×2 cm RGB orthomosaics from 120 m altitude

- Supervised Maximum Likelihood Classification on the drone pixels into 6 classes: Flower, Green Vegetation, Soil, Road, Wood, Shadow

## Slide 6

- Supervised Maximum Likelihood Classifier (MLC) in ENVI software

- Maximum Likelihood Classification assumes each class follows a multivariate Gaussian distribution in feature space (here, R, G, B pixel values)

- Open RGB orthomosaic in ENVI software

- Manually draw training polygons — look at the OM and draws regions of interest (ROIs) for each of the 6 classes: Flower, vegetation, Soil, Road, Wood, Shadow

- The analyst draws maybe dozens of polygons per class, selecting pixels they are confident about.

## Slide 7

- Supervised Maximum Likelihood Classifier (MLC) in ENVI software

- ENVI computes μ, Σ for each class from those training polygons

- each 2cmx2cm pixel gets assigned to the class with highest likelihood

- they withheld 100 "pure ground truth polygons" (also manually drawn, but not used in training) and measured how many pixels were classified correctly → the 96.4% / 89.9% accuracy numbers

- Paper doesnt list exact training polygon count

## Slide 8

- Satellite Data (model input)

- Input (21 features per pixel per prediction date):

- All derived from the Sentinel/PlanetScope GNDVI and EBI time series at anchor dates

- PlanetScope: 3×3 m, near-daily, 4 bands (Blue 455–515 nm, Green 500–590 nm, Red 590–670 nm, NIR 780–860 nm)

- Cloud removal: images where known forest pixels have GNDVI < 0.55 are dropped

- Resampling: 3×3 m → 6×6 m (bicubic) to reduce shadow/noise

- Gap filling: null dates filled with 19-day median moving window

- Smoothing: Savitzky-Golay filter, 19-day window

## Slide 9

- Model

- For any day tx in the flowering window, compute the 21 features from the smoothed satellite time series

- Feed into the RF → get a predicted flowering % for every pixel in the landscape(Output: A single number — % of a 6×6 m satellite pixel's footprint covered by flowers on day tx)

- Pixels below 4% are shown as "no flowering" — detection threshold

## Slide 10

- Input Features

- The 21 input features used in the Random Forest model are a combination of spectral index values at specific points in time, the magnitude and direction of spectral change "vectors," and the raw difference (delta) between those time points

- There are Two Spectral Indices from which these 21 features are made:

- GNDVI (Greenness Index): Measures chlorophyll and canopy health.

- EBI (Flowering Index): Specifically designed to detect cream/white flowering pigments in this paper

- The Five Key Phenological Dates that were used in this paper

- t_base: Represents the pre-budding baseline, indicating the initial state of vegetation before any visible growth or development begins.

- t_start: Denotes the peak budding stage, corresponding to the maximum greenness observed before the onset of flowering.

- t_x: Refers to the specific day of interest for which prediction or analysis is being performed.

- t_fmax: Indicates the date of peak flowering, typically associated with a local minimum in greenness due to the transition from vegetative to reproductive phase.

- t_end: Represents the post-flowering recovery stage, where vegetation begins to regain greenness after flowering.

## Slide 11

- Input Features

- These 21 features are categorised into 3 groups:

- 1.)Raw Spectral Values (9 features):

- GNDVI Values at: t_base, t_start, t_x, t_end, and t_fmax — resulting in a total of 5 features.

- EBI Values at: t_start, t_x, t_end, and t_fmax — resulting in a total of 4 features.

- 2.) The paper uses Change Vector Analysis (CVA)(6 features{magnitude and direction of each}) to describe how the canopy transitions between states. For each vector, the model uses both its Magnitude (strength of change) and Direction (type of change).

- Vector A (Buds to Flowers): Captures the shift from green buds to cream flowers (from t_start to t_x).

- Vector B (Post-Flowering Recovery): Captures the return to a green canopy (from t_x to t_end); this helps filter out drought or fire damage.

- Vector C (Seasonal Trend): Corrects for lighting shifts and sun angles across the whole season (from t_start to t_end).

## Slide 12

- Input Features

Vector A (The Flowering Signal):
 Dates: From t_start (peak budding) to t_x (the day you are analyzing).
 Significance: It quantifies the transition where green buds open into cream flowers. As this happens, greenness (GR) decreases and flowering signal (FL) increases.
 Use: This is the most critical vector for the model; its magnitude is directly related to the quantity of flowers present.

Vector B (The Quality Control Filter):
 Dates: From t_x (day of interest) to t_end (the user-defined post-flowering date).
 Significance: It tracks the canopy "returning to normal" (re-greening) after the flowers fall off.
 Use: It ensures the model doesn't mistake a drought or fire for flowering. If a tree loses greenness but never "re-greens" (fails the Vector B test), the model knows it wasn't a flowering event.

Vector C (The Noise Corrector):
 Dates: From t_start all the way to t_end.
 Significance: It captures the general trend of the entire season.
 Use: It corrects for shifting sun angles and lighting conditions. Over several months, the sun's position changes, which can shift the colors of mixed pixels on the edges of tree crowns; Vector C helps the model ignore this "environmental noise".

## Slide 13

- Input Features

- 3.) Delta (Δ) Change Values (6 features)

- These represent the simple numerical subtraction of index values between the vector dates to help the model see the "drop" in greenness and "spike" in flowering signal.

- Interval A (t_start -> t_x): GNDVI Δ and EBI Δ.

- Interval B (t_end -> t_x): GNDVI Δ and EBI Δ.

- Interval C (t_end -> t_start): GNDVI Δ and EBI Δ.
